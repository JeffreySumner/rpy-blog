Introduction

Welcome back! In Part 1, we gathered images of Hall of Fame eligible players from baseball-reference.com. We scraped hundreds of player photos with one simple goal in mind: to take the phrase “he looks like a superstar” literally.

Today, we’re going to build a neural network that predicts whether a player is a Hall of Famer based solely on their facial features. No stats. No accolades. Just their face. Because apparently, we can judge a book by its cover—or in this case, a baseball player by their headshot.

Let’s dive in!

Image Preprocessing

Neural networks are picky eaters. They want their data in a very specific format. We need to:

  1. Convert images to grayscale (because who needs color when you’re judging faces?)
  2. Resize all images to 32x32 pixels (tiny, but efficient)
  3. Flatten them into vectors for our dense neural network
def load_and_preprocess_image(filename, image_dir):
    """Load an image, convert to grayscale, and resize to 32x32"""
    img_path = image_dir / filename
    img = Image.open(img_path).convert('L')  # Convert to grayscale
    img = img.resize((32, 32))  # Resize to 32x32
    img_array = np.array(img) / 255.0  # Normalize to [0, 1]
    return img_array.flatten()  # Flatten to 1D array

Building the Model

Time to build our neural network! We’re going with a simple architecture:

  • Input layer: 1024 neurons (32x32 pixels)
  • Hidden layer 1: 256 neurons with ReLU activation
  • Hidden layer 2: 128 neurons with ReLU activation
  • Output layer: 2 neurons with softmax activation (HOF or not)

This is a classic dense neural network. Nothing fancy, but it gets the job done.

model = keras.Sequential([
    layers.Dense(256, activation='relu', input_shape=(1024,)),
    layers.Dropout(0.3),
    layers.Dense(128, activation='relu'),
    layers.Dropout(0.3),
    layers.Dense(2, activation='softmax')
])

Training the Model

Let’s compile and train our model. We’ll use binary crossentropy as our loss function and RMSprop as our optimizer.

model.compile(
    loss='binary_crossentropy',
    optimizer='rmsprop',
    metrics=['accuracy']
)

Discussion & Limitations

Let’s be real for a second. This model is ridiculous. And that’s kind of the point!

Why This Doesn’t Actually Work

  1. Survivorship Bias: We only have images of players who were good enough to be considered for the Hall of Fame
  2. Era Effects: Photography styles, image quality, and even facial hair trends have changed over time
  3. Correlation ≠ Causation: Even if the model finds patterns, they’re not causal
  4. Sample Size: We’re working with a relatively small dataset
  5. The Obvious: A player’s face has nothing to do with their baseball ability!

But It’s Still Fun!

Despite all these limitations, this project demonstrates:

  • How to build a simple image classification model
  • The power (and danger) of neural networks finding spurious patterns
  • How to work with image data in Python
  • That sometimes, data science is just for fun

Conclusion

So, can you predict a Hall of Famer by their looks? According to our model… maybe? But should you? Absolutely not!

This project was a fun exercise in taking a common phrase literally and seeing where it leads. We learned about image preprocessing, neural networks, and the importance of thinking critically about what our models are actually learning.

The real takeaway? Just because a model can find patterns doesn’t mean those patterns are meaningful. But hey, at least we had fun along the way!
